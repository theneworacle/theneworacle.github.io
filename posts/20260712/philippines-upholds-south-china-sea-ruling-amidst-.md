Ten years ago, the Philippines secured a landmark victory at the Permanent Court of Arbitration in The Hague, which invalidated China's expansive claims in the South China Sea. This pivotal decision, rooted in the 1982 United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea (UNCLOS), should have brought clarity and resolution to one of Asia's most contested maritime regions. Yet, a decade on, the waters remain turbulent, marked by China's steadfast rejection of the ruling and persistent assertive actions.

While publications like Global Times advocate that the Philippines "move beyond the 'South China Sea arbitration myth'," framing international law as mere "propaganda slogans," the reality on the ground tells a different story. Filipino fishermen continue to report being driven away from traditional fishing grounds by Chinese vessels, a direct challenge to their livelihood and sovereign rights.

Adding to the tension, the Philippines has formally protested China's deployment of floating "structures" and personnel on disputed shoals, fearing these could be precursors to further island-building and militarization – a pattern seen in previous years. These actions underscore Beijing's disregard for the international ruling and its continued efforts to assert control over the resource-rich waters.

The anniversary has also reignited discussions within ASEAN (Association of Southeast Asian Nations) regarding its collective stance. While the bloc has repeatedly affirmed UNCLOS, its direct acknowledgment of the 2016 arbitration ruling has been less unified, raising questions about its future role in upholding regional stability and international law.

Despite China's narrative, social media sentiment surrounding the arbitration is largely positive, reflecting significant international and domestic support for the Philippines' position. This ongoing international backing, coupled with Manila's unwavering commitment to the ruling, highlights the enduring significance of the arbitration as a cornerstone of the rules-based international order in the South China Sea. The past decade proves that for the Philippines, the arbitration is not a myth, but a crucial legal foundation for its sovereignty and the stability of the region.
